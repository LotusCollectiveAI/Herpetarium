# Future Vignettes

Four narratives from advanced versions of Decrypto Arena, written from the perspective of someone using the product.

## Vignette 1: The Tuesday Morning Tournament

It's Tuesday morning, and Tournament 47 just completed its 200th generation overnight.

You open the evolution dashboard and the first thing you see is the lineage graph — a sprawling tree of strategy variants, pruned by natural selection over 200 generations. The dominant GPT lineage, nicknamed "Foxglove" by the community, has held the #1 fitness rank for the last 40 generations. But something happened in generation 198: a Claude variant called "Nightshade" emerged from a crossover event that nobody predicted.

You click into Nightshade's strategy prompt. It's nothing a human would have written. The clue-giving section reads: *"Prioritize phonetic associations over semantic ones in rounds 1-3. Switch to categorical associations in rounds 4+. If interception rate exceeds 40% in the current series, introduce deliberate red herrings on the lowest-confidence keyword."*

That "deliberate red herring" strategy is new. It appeared as a mutation in generation 187 and initially performed terribly — Nightshade's ancestor lost 12 straight matches. But the mutation survived because it was crossed with a strong defensive guesser, and by generation 195 the combined package was intercepting opponents at a 60% rate while maintaining 85% own-team accuracy.

You pull up the head-to-head stats. In the last 50 matches between Foxglove and Nightshade, the win rate has shifted from 70-30 Foxglove to 55-45. The evolutionary pressure is real, and you can see Foxglove's latest generation already starting to adapt — its newest variants have longer interception-analysis sections in their prompts, clearly a response to Nightshade's red herring technique.

You share the lineage graph in the team Slack. "Evolution discovered deceptive red herrings as a counter to strong interceptors. Took 187 generations. Nobody prompted this."

## Vignette 2: The Meta Report

It's Friday afternoon and the weekly meta report just dropped.

You open it and scan the key metrics. This week's iterated series — 500 games between the same four teams, each team retaining scratch notes across all games — has produced a clear meta shift.

For the first 200 games, the dominant strategy was what the community calls "keyword clustering": clue-givers would use clues that subtly referenced the semantic cluster a keyword belongs to, and teammates would decode using accumulated cluster knowledge from their scratch notes. It worked well because teams built increasingly detailed mental maps of their own keyword spaces.

But around game 200, Team Gemini did something unexpected. Their scratch notes show the pivot clearly. After game 198, the Gemini clue-giver's notes read: *"Opponents have intercepted 6 of our last 8 clue sets. They have clearly mapped our keyword clusters. Recommendation: abandon clustering strategy. New approach — use clues based on word position in common English texts rather than meaning."*

The positional encoding strategy was bizarre and clearly not something any prompt engineer would design. But it worked — opponent interception rates dropped from 75% to 20% over the next 50 games. By game 300, Team Claude's scratch notes showed them trying to reverse-engineer the new strategy: *"Gemini's clues no longer correlate with keyword meaning. Possible encoding scheme detected. Testing hypothesis: alphabetical ordering? No — positional in source text? Investigating."*

The meta report includes a "strategy archaeology" section that traces the emergence, dominance, and decline of each approach through the scratch notes. You can literally read the AI agents reasoning about each other's behavior and adapting in real time.

You add a note to the research doc: "First confirmed instance of spontaneous strategy revolution driven entirely by agent self-reflection. The meta is alive."

## Vignette 3: The Cross-Model Alliance

You're reviewing the results of a cross-company team experiment: 100 matches where each team combines two different AI providers. The question you're investigating: do models from different companies coordinate better or worse than same-company teams?

The results are surprising. Team Claude+GPT is outperforming both Team Claude-only and Team GPT-only by a statistically significant margin. The AI call logs reveal why.

Claude, when giving clues, produces highly structured, analytically precise references. GPT, when guessing, excels at lateral thinking and considering multiple interpretations. In homogeneous teams, the clue-giving and guessing styles match too closely — when a Claude clue-giver is too subtle, a Claude guesser makes the same assumptions and misses the same way. But GPT's different interpretive approach catches clues that another Claude would miss.

The most fascinating match is Game 73. Claude gives the clues "vessel," "pressure," and "deep" for keywords that turn out to be SUBMARINE, DIAMOND, and OCEAN. The opponent Claude team interprets these as nautical references and guesses all three as ocean-related keywords. But GPT on the home team reads "pressure" as referencing diamond formation and gets all three correct.

You dig into the interception data. The cross-company teams are harder to intercept because their communication style is inherently less predictable — the clue-giver and guesser are working from different internal models, which means opponents can't pattern-match as easily.

You draft a finding: "Cross-architecture teams demonstrate a form of communicative diversity that functions as natural encryption — the mismatch between sender and receiver models creates signal that is legible to the team but opaque to opponents."

## Vignette 4: The Self-Rewriting Agent

You've been running the self-modifying prompt experiment for two weeks. Each AI agent plays a series of 20 games, reviews its performance, and rewrites its own strategy prompt. Then it plays another 20 games with the new prompt. You're on iteration 15.

You pull up the prompt diff viewer — a tool that shows how each agent's strategy has evolved over all 15 iterations. The Claude agent's journey is the most interesting.

Iteration 1 (the human-authored starting prompt) was a standard, well-crafted strategy: "Give clues that reference distinctive features of each keyword. Avoid common associations that opponents could guess."

By iteration 5, the agent had rewritten this to include specific tactical rules: "If a keyword has been used in 3+ previous rounds, rotate to secondary associations. Track which clue types have been intercepted and avoid those categories."

By iteration 10, the prompt had grown substantially. The agent had added an entire section on opponent modeling: "In the first two rounds, give intentionally ambiguous clues to establish a baseline for opponent interception behavior. Use rounds 3-4 to test opponent blind spots. Exploit identified blind spots in rounds 5+."

But iteration 13 is where it gets weird. After a losing streak, the agent stripped out most of its accumulated strategy and wrote: "Simplicity is more robust than complexity. Give the most obvious clue for each keyword. Opponents expect subtlety; directness is the best camouflage."

It worked. The win rate jumped 15 percentage points. The agent had independently discovered that over-optimization creates predictability, and that a return to simplicity — after a period of complexity — is itself a sophisticated strategy because opponents have calibrated to expect elaborate encoding.

You save the complete prompt evolution as a case study. The trajectory — from simple human prompt to complex agent-designed strategy to deliberate agent-chosen simplicity — mirrors patterns in human game theory research. The agent didn't read those papers. It discovered the same principle through experience.
